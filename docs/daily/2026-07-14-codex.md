## Key Updates

- **Harness economics became the clearest production-agent signal**: the new "Harness Effect" paper argues that orchestration design, not model choice alone, sets enterprise agent cost curves; in its controlled 22-task comparison, changing only the harness cut blended cost per task by 41%, wall-clock time by 44%, and tokens by 38% while preserving task quality. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 release pushed the same theme from the model/API side: programmatic tool calling, multi-agent `ultra`, and long-horizon agent benchmarks are being framed around useful work per token, not raw intelligence alone ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06906), [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-6/)).

- **LangSmith/Fleet continued hardening the agent control plane**: the latest LangSmith changelog emphasized human-in-the-loop and enterprise-runtime details, including better subagent approval prompts, private skill loading in fine-grained-access workspaces, Slack daily-brief/@mention support for Fleet templates, and self-hosted evaluator/playground proxy handling with SSRF validation. The direction is practical: agents need governed skills, approvals, channel integration, and self-host/private-cloud compatibility before they can own real workflows ([LangSmith changelog](https://docs.langchain.com/langsmith/changelog)).

- **Evaluation discourse moved away from pass/fail leaderboards**: OpenAI's SWE-Bench Pro audit estimated roughly 30% of the public split had broken or problematic tasks, while AgentLens proposed production-assessed trajectory reviews for coding agents, evaluating instruction following, tool use, verification, recovery, and communication across the whole run. For production teams, this reinforces that agent evals must inspect traces and task validity, not just final test status ([OpenAI eval audit](https://openai.com/index/separating-signal-from-noise-coding-evaluations/), [AgentLens](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06624)).

## Hot Discussions

- **Enterprise agent adoption is converging on infrastructure modernization plus FDE-style deployment**: Google Cloud's recent survey coverage reported that 83% of senior IT leaders believe infrastructure upgrades are needed for production-grade agentic AI, with hidden inference, governance, and operational-complexity costs as major blockers. Google Cloud and Accenture's mid-market push pairs agentic solutions with Forward Deployed Engineers, matching the broader realization that workflow redesign and embedded deployment expertise are the scarce layer ([TechRadar](https://www.techradar.com/pro/the-gap-between-ai-ambition-and-infrastructure-reality-is-widening-google-cloud-report-finds-83-percent-of-organizations-must-overhaul-their-infrastructure-in-order-to-maximize-the-agentic-ai-opportunity), [ITPro](https://www.itpro.com/business/business-strategy/ai-projects-are-stalling-at-mid-market-firms-google-cloud-and-accenture-want-to-solve-that)).

- **Real-world adoption stories favored "agentic pods" over generic rollout programs**: Uber's CTO described embedding 30 AI-proficient engineers into HR, finance, and legal teams to observe actual work and build agents, producing examples such as financial pacing reports falling from two days to 10 minutes and capital allocation across 150 cities dropping from 15 hours to 30 minutes. The interesting pattern is not the time savings alone; it is the FDE/PDE loop of observation, workflow redesign, agent buildout, and repeatable internal scale-out ([Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/uber-cto-bets-on-agentic-pods-make-ai-more-efficient-2026-7)).

## Actionable Insight

- Treat the next internal agent pilot as a harness-and-deployment program: define trace schema, task-validity checks, approval rules, scoped tool/memory access, cost-per-completed-task telemetry, and a small FDE-style team that can observe real workflows before automating them. For private-cloud or regulated environments, the defensible asset is the owned eval corpus plus governed runtime, not the first demo agent.
